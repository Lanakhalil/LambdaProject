Human Vision

The human's vision system is the largest sensory system, making up to 70% of all the sensory receptors in the human body! The physiology of the vision process is even more fascinating, get ready to be thrilled.

Vision is initiated when light incidents on the eye, and through multiple steps it is then translated as nerval signal that can be understood by the human brain.
First, the light passes through the cornea, as it does, it's bent or refracted onto the lens. As the light passes through the lens, it's refracted a second time (note that the lens is biconvex in the human eye). This 'double-bending' causes the image to be reversed (turned upside-down). If that was the end of the story, the world would always seem upside down, fortunately, it is turned around in the brain.
Before it reaches the brain, the image must travel as pulses through the optical nurve. This process is produced in the retina, which consists of the pigmented layer and neural layer. The photochemical reaction happens in the pigmented layer and a voltage is produced, then the neural layer transfers all the data to the optical nerve.
After these pulses reach the brain, the signals are translated into what we experience as vision. If that was not enough to excite you, wait until you learn about how we capture colors, using our excited photoreceptors.
